In Memoriam

Games

Overview

In Memoriam is a story driven 3D action platformer built in a team of four for Unity for Simon Fraser University’s Foundations of Game Design class. The aim of the project was to create a game with a branching narrative structure while applying concepts such as player types as well as core and satellite mechanics.

Roles: Lead Programmer | Gameplay Designer

Timeline: Feb - Mar 2025

Tools: Unity 3D & Version Control | Figma

Goal:
Apply learned knowledge of narrative structures, level design, core and satellite mechanics, and reward scheduling to create a game that integrates narrative and gameplay into a branching narrative.

Pre-production Art and Narrative Exploration

Prior to production, I contributed to the art of In Memoriam with a concept design for the player character (as shown in figure 1), and some rough storyboards for narrative beats (as shown in figures 2 and 3). Although my character design did not make it through to the end, elements of the design such as the wolf metaphor and clothing genre were kept.

Development Phase 1

My focus initially was to implement the foundational mechanics of our game as an action platformer: movement and camera controls. From here, I moved onto combat, health, and enemy behaviours (as shown in figure 4) that could be inherited from to create a variety of enemies.

Although the game’s scope was relatively small, I wanted to create an easily expandable system to make every enemy different. Figure 5 shows the result, a completed enemy and its editable properties. Were I to work on this project further, I would easily be able to add more unique behaviours to each enemy such as more complicated or unique attacks.

Development Phase 2

Outside of programming, I was also the most experienced in using Unity and did much of the work bringing over the art and animation created by our group's lead artist. Figure 6 shows some of this process as I use the sprites created by the team’s artist.

Challenges

Because the player is 2D in a 3D environment and we only had sprites from one camera perspective, playtesters struggled to gauge where the player was going to land after a jump. To fix this, I moved the player character’s light closer to their feet so players could use it as a shadow. This change can be seen when comparing Figures 7 and 8. This somewhat decreased the issue however, If I continued with the project, I would include sprites from different camera angles as well.

Outcome

Developing In Memoriam put my programming and Unity skills to the test. Designing and programming the game’s mechanics was a big undertaking and using playtesting to improve the project helped me take away a lot from this project.
